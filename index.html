What is TC39 responsible for?

  • To maintain and update the standard for the ECMAScript programming language.
  • To identify, develop and maintain standards for libraries that extend the capabilities of ECMAScript.
  • To develop test suites that may be used to verify correct implementation of these standards.
  • To contribute selected standards to ISO/IEC JTC 1.
  • To evaluate and consider proposals for complementary or additional technologies.

TC39 - Who is in it?

Members of ECMA are not individuals, they are organizations and corporations.

As such, the members of the Technical committee 39 are organizations, not individuals, but it is attended by individuals representing those organizations.

TC39 - what do they actually do?

They meet on a regular basis and review proposals that are made for the language. They standardize the language and ensure that it conforms to certain requirements

What does a proposal look like?

It is a long process, and is done in four (five) parts!

Stage 0: Strawman

Got an idea? Here is how you start:

You will need to find a TC39 Champion (someone on the committee) to pick up your idea and presents it. You can get into this process in a few ways. By registering on ECMA international, or via esdiscuss

At this stage you need a good idea, not necessarily a demo (yet)

if your idea isn't rejected, then you can continue working on it!

expect a LOT of changes and critiques

Stage 1: Proposal

Ok. You made it past the first stage, now you need a demo!

In this stage you create an initial draft, often with a lot of feedback

You will need to create an implementation that shows the proposed behavior, often as a polyfill or something similar.

expect some significant changes

Stage 2: Draft

Woo! Draft stage! You have a demo, and you are starting to get specific

all major semantics, syntax and API are covered, but TODOs, placeholders and editorial issues are expected

Technical Committee 39 of ECMA International

This means, you will have to write a language spec! This means describing the semantics and syntax using formal spec language.

expect some (but often minor) changes

Stage 3: Candidate

Congratz! you have a Candidate. That means that it now needs input from a LOT of users. It needs to go out into the wild. This might be as a browser implementation or similar. You need lots of feedback from people who use your new feature.

You will also need to write test262 acceptance tests, so that those implementing your feature in the future will be able to run them against your tests.

expect critical changes which will probably be suggested by people based on their implementation experience

Stage 4: Finished

Wow, that probably took a long time. But the feature is done!

Now, it is up to browsers and engine writers to implement your feature. It may still be some time before users use your new feature!
